Guides that actually help.

Real, practical guides for situations teens face — written in plain language, not therapy-speak.

You don't need a serious reason or a crisis to visit your school counselor. They're there for stress, friendship problems, family stuff, feeling off — all of it.

How to get an appointment:

  • Walk into the counseling office and ask for a time to talk
  • Email or message them if walking in feels too hard
  • Ask a teacher or the front office to help set it up

What to say if you don't know how to start: "I've been feeling really stressed/anxious/sad lately and I just needed someone to talk to." That's enough.

About privacy: School counselors keep most things confidential. They typically only involve parents if they believe you're in immediate danger. You can ask them directly: "What would you have to tell my parents about?"

💡 You can literally say "I've never done this before and I'm not sure what to expect." They hear this all the time — it's a perfectly good way to start.

This one can feel the scariest. But many parents, when they hear their kid is struggling, respond with love — even if their first reaction is imperfect.

Choose the right moment: Not right after school during a chaotic moment. Try a quiet evening, a car ride, or after dinner. Calmer context, calmer conversation.

  • "I've been having a hard time lately and I think I need some help."
  • "I wanted to tell you I've been struggling with [anxiety/sadness/stress] — can we talk about getting some support?"
  • "I don't need you to fix it. I just need you to know what's going on."

If it doesn't go well at first: Give it time. Try again, or try the other parent, or another trusted adult — an aunt, uncle, or older sibling.

💡 Writing it in a text or note can be easier than saying it out loud. There is absolutely no shame in that.

Finding a therapist can feel overwhelming, but it's more doable than it seems once you know where to look.

Best places to search:

  • Psychology Today — search by teen, insurance, specialty, and location
  • Open Path Collective — licensed therapists at $30–$80 per session
  • Search "therapists for teens near [your city]" on Google
  • Ask your school counselor — they often have a referral list ready

What to look for: Someone who specializes in what you're dealing with. Many offer free 15-minute intro calls — use them to feel out the fit.

It's okay to switch: If a therapist doesn't feel right after a few sessions, you can find another one. This is completely normal.

💡 When emailing: "I'm a teen looking for support with [anxiety/depression/etc.]. Do you have availability and take [insurance / sliding scale]?"

The first session is mostly getting to know each other. Think of it less like a test and more like a first conversation with someone new.

What your therapist will probably do:

  • Ask general questions about your life, family, school, and interests
  • Ask what brought you in and what you're hoping to work on
  • Explain how they work and what confidentiality means for you

You don't have to share everything right away. It's okay to say "I'm not ready to talk about that yet." A good therapist will never push.

It might feel awkward — that's completely normal. Showing up is enough. You don't have to say the right things.

💡 Give it at least 3–4 sessions before deciding if a therapist is the right fit. Trust takes time to build.

Therapy can be expensive, but there are more options than most people realize.

  • School counselors — completely free, already at your school
  • Community mental health centers — search "[your city] community mental health". Sliding scale fees based on income
  • University training clinics — supervised student therapists at very low cost
  • Open Path Collective — $30–$80/session with licensed therapists
  • 7 Cups — free volunteer listener chats, always available
  • Woebot — free CBT-based app for daily support

If you have insurance: Call the number on your card and ask "What mental health benefits are covered?" Many plans cover therapy at low or no cost.

💡 It's okay to ask a therapist directly: "Do you offer a sliding scale for teens?" Many say yes — you just have to ask.

When someone you care about is hurting, it can be hard to know what to do. The most important thing is to show up — not to fix it.

What to say:

  • "I'm here for you. You don't have to go through this alone."
  • "I don't fully understand, but I care about you."
  • "Is it okay if I check in on you?"

What not to do: Don't say "it could be worse" or "just think positive." Don't promise to keep dangerous secrets.

If you're worried about their safety: If a friend talks about hurting themselves, tell a trusted adult. This is not betrayal — it's the most caring thing you can do.

💡 You can text 988 not just for yourself, but to ask how to help a friend who might be in crisis.

Who we are

Our mission.

We don't provide therapy — we help you find it. Here's why we built this, and how it works.

Guiding Light Youth was created because navigating mental health as a teenager is hard — not just emotionally, but logistically. Where do you go? Who do you call? What if you can't afford it? What if your parents don't understand?

We've done the research so you don't have to start from scratch. Every resource here is real and publicly available.

This is not a therapy service. We help you find one.

We are not affiliated with any listed service and earn nothing from referrals. Questions or suggestions welcome.
